Cellular respiration is the process by which biological fuels are oxidized in the presence of an inorganic electron acceptor, such as oxygen, to produce large amounts of energy, to drive the bulk production of ATP.

The overall reaction for cellular respiration, when using glucose as the primary metabolic fuel, is typically written as:

C6H12O6 + 6O2 → 6CO2 + 6H2O + ATP + heat

This process consists of three main stages: Glycolysis, the Citric Acid Cycle (Krebs Cycle), and Oxidative Phosphorylation.

1. Glycolysis

Occurring in the cytoplasm, glycolysis involves the breakdown of one molecule of glucose (a six-carbon compound) into two molecules of pyruvate (a three-carbon compound). This anaerobic process nets 2 ATP molecules and 2 NADH molecules per glucose molecule.

The regulatory enzyme phosphofructokinase-1 (PFK-1) is the committed step of the pathway, heavily regulated by the cell's energy charge (ATP/AMP ratio).

2. Pyruvate Oxidation & The Citric Acid Cycle

Upon entering the mitochondrial matrix, pyruvate is oxidized to Acetyl-CoA by the Pyruvate Dehydrogenase Complex. Acetyl-CoA then enters the Citric Acid Cycle, combining with oxaloacetate to form citrate.

Through a series of redox reactions, the cycle produces 3 NADH, 1 FADH2, and 1 GTP (which can be converted to ATP) per turn. Since one glucose yields two pyruvates, the cycle turns twice per glucose molecule.

The Big Picture: Energy Conversion

Think of cellular respiration as the cell's power plant. It takes raw fuel (glucose) and slowly extracts the energy to charge up the cell's "batteries" (ATP). It doesn't release all the energy at once—that would be an explosion—but rather in small, controlled steps.

Key Regulatory Step: PFK-1

Phosphofructokinase-1 (PFK-1) acts as the "thermostat" for glycolysis. When the cell has plenty of energy (high ATP), PFK-1 slows down the pathway. When energy is low (high AMP), it speeds it up. This prevents the cell from wasting glucose when it doesn't need energy.
